Moving Guide to Pennsylvania

Everything you need to know about relocating to the Keystone State — neighborhoods, costs, schools, and insider tips.

Why People Are Moving to Pennsylvania

Pennsylvania offers a rare combination of urban energy and suburban tranquility, world-class healthcare and education, and a cost of living that's considerably lower than neighboring states like New Jersey and New York. Whether you're relocating for work, family, or a fresh start, the state has something for everyone.

Philadelphia, the state's largest city, has experienced a renaissance in recent years — with booming restaurant scenes, revitalized neighborhoods, and a thriving job market in healthcare, education, tech, and finance. But Pennsylvania's appeal extends well beyond Philly, with vibrant communities in the Lehigh Valley, Poconos, Central PA, and Western PA.

Top Regions to Consider

Philadelphia

Diverse neighborhoods, walkable culture, top hospitals, and a median home price well below other major East Coast cities. Ideal for young professionals, families, and investors.

Philadelphia Suburbs

Montgomery, Bucks, Delaware, and Chester counties offer top-rated schools, larger homes, and easy access to the city via SEPTA regional rail.

Lehigh Valley

Allentown, Bethlehem, and Easton form a growing metro with affordable housing, a strong job market, and a vibrant arts and dining scene.

Poconos & Beyond

Mountain living with year-round recreation, remote work potential, and home prices that are a fraction of what you'd pay closer to the city.

Cost of Living Snapshot

Pennsylvania's cost of living is roughly 3–5% below the national average, depending on the region. Housing is the biggest savings — median home prices in Philadelphia are roughly half of what you'd pay in New York City or Boston, and suburban options offer even more value.

Other key cost factors: Pennsylvania has a flat state income tax of 3.07%, which is lower than most neighboring states. There's no sales tax on groceries or clothing. Property taxes vary by county but are generally reasonable compared to New Jersey.

Schools & Education

Pennsylvania is home to some of the nation's best public and private schools, from the top-rated suburban districts of Lower Merion, Radnor, and Central Bucks to Philadelphia's magnet school system. The state also boasts a dense network of colleges and universities — including Penn, Temple, Drexel, Villanova, and Lehigh.

Getting Around

Philadelphia has the best public transit system in the state, with SEPTA buses, subways, trolleys, and regional rail. The Pennsylvania Turnpike connects the major regions east-to-west, and Amtrak's Northeast Corridor makes trips to New York and DC quick and easy.

For those moving outside the city, a car is the most practical option — but commute times are generally reasonable compared to other major metro areas.
